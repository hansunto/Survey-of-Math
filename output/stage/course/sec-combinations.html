    Section Combinations

    Definition 4.53. Combination.

    A combination of items occurs when:
    • The items are selected from the same group.
      🔗
      🔗
    • No item is used more than once.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The order of items makes no difference.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 4.54. Distinguishing Permutations and Combinations.

    For each of the following, determine whether the problem involves permutations or combinations:
    🔗
    1. How many ways can you select 6 free videos from a list of 200 videos?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. In a race with 50 runners and no ties, in how many ways can the first three finishers come in?
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Baskin-Robbins offers 31 flavors. How many different three-scoop bowls (each a different flavor) are possible?
      🔗
      🔗
    4. A three-person committee is needed from a board of six supervisors. How many different committees can be formed?
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. Combination: The order in which you pick the videos doesn’t change the set you get to watch.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Permutation: Order matters because 1st, 2nd, and 3rd place are different rankings.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Combination: A bowl of Chocolate, Vanilla, and Strawberry is the same as Strawberry, Vanilla, and Chocolate.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Combination: On a committee, all members have equal status; the order of selection does not change the group’s makeup.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 4.55. The Combination Formula.

    The number of combinations of \(n\) things taken \(r\) at a time is denoted by \({}_n C_r\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} {}_n C_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!r!} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.56. Public Transportation Committee.

    A three-person committee is needed to study ways of improving public transportation. How many committees could be formed from the eight people on the board of supervisors?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since this is a committee where roles are not specified, the order does not matter. We use the combination formula with \(n=8\) and \(r=3\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} {}_8 C_3 = \frac{8!}{(8-3)!3!} = \frac{8!}{5!3!} \end{equation*}
    Expanding the factorials:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{8 \cdot 7 \cdot 6 \cdot 5!}{5! \cdot (3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1)} = \frac{336}{6} = 56 \end{equation*}
    There are 56 possible committees.
    🔗

    Example 4.57. The 107th Congress Committee.

    The U.S. Senate of the 107th Congress consisted of 50 Democrats, 49 Republicans, and one independent. How many committees can be formed if each committee must have 3 Democrats and 2 Republicans?
    🔗
    Solution.
    We must choose the Democrats and the Republicans separately and then use the Fundamental Counting Principle to multiply them.
    🔗
    Step 1: Choose 3 Democrats from 50:
    \begin{equation*} {}_{50} C_3 = \frac{50 \cdot 49 \cdot 48}{3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1} = 19,600 \end{equation*}
    Step 2: Choose 2 Republicans from 49:
    \begin{equation*} {}_{49} C_2 = \frac{49 \cdot 48}{2 \cdot 1} = 1,176 \end{equation*}
    Step 3: Multiply the results:
    \begin{equation*} 19,600 \cdot 1,176 = 23,049,600 \end{equation*}
    There are 23,049,600 possible committees.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.58. Lottery Selections.

    To win a Mega Millions lottery, you must pick 5 numbers from a collection of 56, and one Megaball number from a collection of 46. The order of the first 5 numbers does not matter. How many different selections are possible?
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we calculate the combinations for the 5 numbers:
    \begin{equation*} {}_{56} C_5 = \frac{56!}{51!5!} = 3,819,816 \end{equation*}
    Next, there are 46 choices for the single Megaball. We multiply these together:
    \begin{equation*} 3,819,816 \cdot 46 = 175,711,536 \end{equation*}
    There are over 175 million possible number combinations.
