    Section Fundamentals of Probability

    Definition 4.1. Probability Formula.

    The theoretical probability of an event is a measure of how likely that event is to occur based on the assumption that all outcomes in the sample space are equally likely.
    🔗
    If an event E has \(n(E)\) equally likely outcomes and its sample space S has \(n(S)\) equally likely outcomes, the probability of event, \(P(E)\text{,}\) is
    \begin{gather*} P(E) = \frac{\text{number of outcomes in event } E}{\text{total number of outcomes in sample space}} = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)} \end{gather*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.2. Probability of a Single Die Roll.

    A die is rolled. The set of equally likely outcomes is \(\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}\text{.}\) Find the probability of rolling:
    🔗
    1. a 2
      🔗
      🔗
    2. a number less than 4
      🔗
      🔗
    3. a number greater than 7
      🔗
      🔗
    4. a number less than 7
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. \(\displaystyle P(2) = \frac{1}{6}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The outcomes are \(\{1, 2, 3\}\text{,}\) so \(P(\text{less than 4}) = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. There are no such outcomes, so \(P(\text{greater than 7}) = \frac{0}{6} = 0\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. All outcomes are less than 7, so \(P(\text{less than 7}) = \frac{6}{6} = 1\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.3. Probability of a Pair of Dice.

    A single die is rolled twice. Find the probability of rolling:
    🔗
    1. two even numbers
      🔗
      🔗
    2. two numbers whose sum is 6
      🔗
      🔗
    3. two numbers whose sum exceeds 12
      🔗
      🔗
    4. two prime numbers
      🔗
      🔗
    5. two numbers whose sum is less than 7
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. The even numbers on a die are \(\{2, 4, 6\}\text{.}\) There are \(3 \times 3 = 9\) outcomes where both numbers are even. The probability is \(\frac{9}{36} = \frac{1}{4},\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The outcomes with a sum of 6 are \(\{(1,5), (2,4), (3,3), (4,2), (5,1)\}\text{.}\) There are 5 such outcomes, so the probability is \(\frac{5}{36}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The maximum possible sum is \(6 + 6 = 12\text{.}\) It is impossible for the sum to exceed 12. The probability is \(\frac{0}{36} = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. The prime numbers on a die are \(\{2, 3, 5\}\text{.}\) There are \(3 \times 3 = 9\) outcomes where both numbers are prime. The probability is \(\frac{9}{36} = \frac{1}{4}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. The outcomes with a sum less than 7 are:
      • Sum 2: (1,1) [1 outcome]
        🔗
      • Sum 3: (1,2), (2,1) [2 outcomes]
        🔗
      • Sum 4: (1,3), (2,2), (3,1) [3 outcomes]
        🔗
      • Sum 5: (1,4), (2,3), (3,2), (4,1) [4 outcomes]
        🔗
      • Sum 6: (1,5), (2,4), (3,3), (4,2), (5,1) [5 outcomes]
        🔗
      Total outcomes = \(1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 = 15\text{.}\) The probability is \(\frac{15}{36} = \frac{5}{12}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.5. Probability of Drawing a Single deck of Cards.

    You are dealt one card from a standard 52-card deck. Find the probability of being dealt:
    🔗
    1. an ace
      🔗
      🔗
    2. a red card
      🔗
      🔗
    3. a red king
      🔗
      🔗
    4. a spade
      🔗
      🔗
    5. an even number heart
      🔗
      🔗
    6. an odd number diamond
      🔗
      🔗
    7. a face card
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    There are 52 total outcomes in a standard deck.
    🔗
    1. There are 4 aces in a deck. \(P(\text{ace}) = \frac{4}{52} = \frac{1}{13}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. There are 26 red cards (hearts and diamonds). \(P(\text{red}) = \frac{26}{52} = \frac{1}{2}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. There are 2 red kings (king of hearts and king of diamonds). \(P(\text{red king}) = \frac{2}{52} = \frac{1}{26}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. There are 13 spades in a deck. \(P(\text{spade}) = \frac{13}{52} = \frac{1}{4}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. The even numbered hearts are \(\{2, 4, 6, 8, 10\}\text{.}\) There are 5 such cards. \(P(\text{even heart}) = \frac{5}{52}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    6. The odd numbered diamonds (excluding face cards) are \(\{3, 5, 7, 9\}\text{.}\) (Note: Ace is usually not considered an odd number in this context, but if included, the count would be 5). Using \(\{3, 5, 7, 9\}\text{,}\) there are 4 such cards. \(P(\text{odd diamond}) = \frac{4}{52} = \frac{1}{13}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    7. There are 12 face cards (Jack, Queen, King of each suit). \(P(\text{face card}) = \frac{12}{52} = \frac{3}{13}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.8. Probability of Three Coin Tosses.

    A fair coin is tossed three times in succession. The set of equally likely outcomes is \(\{HHH, HHT, HTH, HTT, THH, THT, TTH, TTT\}\text{.}\) Find the probability of getting:
    🔗
    1. Exactly one tail.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Exactly 2 heads.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. At least one head.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Five tails.
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    The total number of outcomes is 8.
    🔗
    1. The outcomes with exactly one tail are \(\{HHT, HTH, THH\}\text{.}\) There are 3 such outcomes, so \(P(\text{exactly one tail}) = \frac{3}{8}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The outcomes with exactly two heads are \(\{HHT, HTH, THH\}\text{.}\) There are 3 such outcomes, so \(P(\text{exactly 2 heads}) = \frac{3}{8}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The only outcome with no heads is \(\{TTT\}\text{.}\) Therefore, there are \(8 - 1 = 7\) outcomes with at least one head. \(P(\text{at least one head}) = \frac{7}{8}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. It is impossible to get five tails in only three tosses. \(P(\text{five tails}) = \frac{0}{8} = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 4.10. Probability of a Coin Toss and Die Roll.

    A coin is tossed and a die is rolled. The tree diagram below illustrates the sample space of 12 equally likely outcomes.
    🔗
    Find the probability of getting:
    🔗
    1. a head and an even number.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. a tail and a number less than 3.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. a head and a number greater than 6.
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    The total number of equally likely outcomes is \(2 \times 6 = 12\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. The outcomes for a head and an even number are \(\{(H, 2), (H, 4), (H, 6)\}\text{.}\) There are 3 such outcomes, so \(P(\text{head and even}) = \frac{3}{12} = \frac{1}{4}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The outcomes for a tail and a number less than 3 are \(\{(T, 1), (T, 2)\}\text{.}\) There are 2 such outcomes, so \(P(\text{tail and less than 3}) = \frac{2}{12} = \frac{1}{6}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Since a standard die only goes up to 6, it is impossible to roll a number greater than 6. The probability is \(\frac{0}{12} = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
